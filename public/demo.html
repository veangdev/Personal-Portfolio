Phishing & Social Engineering

Attackers exploit human psychology ‚Äî not firewalls. Awareness is the #1 defense.

üé£

Phishing

Mass deceptive emails mimicking trusted brands ‚Äî banks, Microsoft, HR ‚Äî to steal credentials or install malware through malicious links or attachments.

üéØ

Spear-Phishing

Targeted attacks using victim's name, role, or colleagues to appear legitimate. Far more convincing and dangerous than generic mass phishing campaigns.

üé≠

Pretexting

Attacker fabricates a scenario ‚Äî "I'm IT support, I need your login to fix a critical issue" ‚Äî to psychologically manipulate the victim into compliance.

"Employees are targeted because they hold system access and aren't always trained to question urgency-driven requests."

When Phishing Succeeded

Part 2 ¬∑ Password & Authentication Threats

Password & Authentication Threats

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Vulnerabilities

  • Weak passwords ‚Äî "123456" cracked in under 1 second by brute-force tools.
  • Password reuse ‚Äî one breached site exposes all accounts ("credential stuffing").
  • Dictionary attacks ‚Äî automated tools cycle millions of common words instantly.
  • Default credentials ‚Äî "admin/admin" shipped with devices and never changed.

ü§ù Credential Sharing Risks

  • Removes individual accountability ‚Äî impossible to trace who did what.
  • Complicates security audits and access reviews.
  • One disgruntled employee can compromise all shared accounts.

üì° Unsecured Authentication

Public Wi-Fi or plain HTTP logins expose credentials to Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) attacks ‚Äî attackers intercept traffic invisibly and silently.

Part 2 ¬∑ Email & Communication Safety

Email & Communication Safety

Email is the #1 attack vector. These 5 rules are your frontline defense.

01

Verify sender address ‚Äî check the full email, not just the display name. "HR Dept" can hide attacker@evil.com.

02

Hover before clicking ‚Äî preview the actual destination URL before any click on any link.

03

Question urgency ‚Äî "Act NOW or lose access!" is a manipulation tactic, not a real IT warning.

04

Spot errors ‚Äî grammar mistakes, odd logos, or strange formatting signal a phishing attempt.

05

Never share credentials ‚Äî no legitimate service ever requests passwords by email.

üì¢ Why Reporting to IT Matters

One report lets IT block malicious domains for the entire organization ‚Äî one person's vigilance protects everyone.

Part 3 ¬∑ Physical Security Threats

Physical Security Threats

üö∂

Tailgating

Unauthorized persons follow badge-holding staff through secure doors ‚Äî then freely access server rooms, plant keyloggers, or intercept sensitive data.

üíª

Unattended Devices

30 seconds with an unlocked laptop is enough to install malware via USB, harvest saved credentials, or copy confidential files with no trace left behind.

üëÄ

Shoulder Surfing

Watching screens or keypads in cafes, airports, or open offices captures passwords and sensitive data in real time ‚Äî requires zero technical skill.

üîó How Physical Breaches Escalate to Digital Disasters

Physical access bypasses every digital defense. A stolen laptop holds cached credentials and VPN configs. A tailgater reaching the server room can plug in a rogue device that silently exfiltrates data for months.

Part 3 ¬∑ Scenario-Based Response

The HR Phishing Email Scenario

An employee receives an email appearing to be from HR, asking them to click a link to update payroll information. It has the company logo but contains grammatical errors and an unfamiliar sender address.

Q1

Verify legitimacy: Inspect the full sender address. Do NOT click. Call HR on a known number. Forward to IT Security for analysis before any action.

Q2

Already clicked? Change the password immediately. Notify IT. Activate MFA now. Monitor all accounts for suspicious activity. File an incident report.

Q3

Prevention: Deploy AI email filtering. Enforce MFA everywhere. Implement DMARC/SPF/DKIM. Run regular phishing simulations for all staff.

Part 4 ¬∑ Policies & Leadership

Policies, Enforcement & Leadership

üìú Key Security Policies

  • Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Defines what employees may do with systems and devices.
  • Incident Reporting Policy: Clear steps to report breaches ‚Äî turning days of delay into minutes.
  • Data Classification Policy: Labels data Public / Internal / Confidential / Secret.
  • BYOD Policy: Governs personal devices accessing corporate networks.

‚öñÔ∏è Security vs. Productivity

Use SSO to reduce friction. Co-create policies with employees for buy-in. Make the secure path the easiest path.

üëî Why Leadership Is the Foundation

  • Sets the tone ‚Äî when executives follow policies, everyone else does too.
  • Controls budget ‚Äî tools, training, and response plans need executive sign-off.
  • Drives accountability ‚Äî security becomes a business priority, not just IT's burden.
  • Builds trust ‚Äî a blame-free culture encourages faster incident reporting.

"Security is not the IT department's problem. It is every leader's responsibility." ‚Äî NIST CSF 2.0

Part 5 ¬∑ Password Management & MFA

Password Management & MFA

‚úÖ 3 Best Practices

  • Use long passphrases (16+ chars) ‚Äî "Coffee!Laptop#2024" beats "P@ss1" every time.
  • Unique password per account ‚Äî never reuse. One breach must never cascade.
  • Change immediately after any suspected compromise ‚Äî don't delay.

üóÇÔ∏è Password Managers

Bitwarden, 1Password, KeePass ‚Äî generate and store complex unique passwords. One master password, zero reuse risk.

üì± Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

A second layer attackers can't bypass even with your password:

  • Something you know ‚Äî password or PIN
  • Something you have ‚Äî authenticator app, SMS code, YubiKey hardware token
  • Something you are ‚Äî fingerprint or facial recognition

MFA blocks 99.9% of automated attacks ‚Äî a stolen password alone is useless. ‚Äî Microsoft, 2023

Part 5 ¬∑ Device & Data Protection

Device & Data Protection

üíª Securing Work Devices

  • Enable full-disk encryption ‚Äî BitLocker (Windows) or FileVault (Mac)
  • Lock screen instantly when stepping away ‚Äî Win+L or 60-sec auto-lock
  • Keep OS & software updated ‚Äî patches close actively exploited vulnerabilities
  • Use a VPN on any remote or public network
  • Only install IT-approved software ‚Äî shadow IT introduces unvetted risk
  • Enable remote wipe for lost or stolen devices

üóÇÔ∏è Clean Desk Policy

Clear all sensitive materials from your desk at end of each day. Prevents:

  • Unauthorized viewing by visitors or cleaning staff
  • Overnight document or device theft
  • Post-it note passwords ‚Äî the most common physical vulnerability
  • Data exposure during unauthorized after-hours access

"If it's confidential, lock it away ‚Äî don't leave it for the cleaning crew to find."
